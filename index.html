6.1

Basic ICT Concepts

Data and information, evolution of ICT, digital devices, communication systems

6.1.1

Data & Information

Data refers to raw, unorganised facts and figures — numbers, words, symbols, or signals — that carry no meaning by themselves until they are processed. For example, the numbers 78, 85, 92, 67 are simply data.

Information is data that has been processed, organised, structured, or placed in a context that makes it meaningful and useful to the person receiving it. “The class average mark is 80.5” is information derived from the raw data above.

The transformation of data into information is called data processing, and it typically follows an Input → Process → Output cycle. Good information should be accurate, timely, relevant, and complete.

ICT theory often extends this idea into the DIKW hierarchy: Data → Information → Knowledge → Wisdom — where Knowledge is information combined with experience and context, and Wisdom is the applied judgement to use that knowledge well.

Key facts & history
  • Data = raw facts; Information = processed, meaningful data
  • Data processing cycle: Input → Process → Output
  • DIKW hierarchy: Data, Information, Knowledge, Wisdom
  • Qualities of good information: accurate, timely, relevant, complete
6.1.2

Evolution of ICT

Long before electronics, humans used mechanical aids to calculate. The Abacus (used from roughly 2700–2300 BCE in Mesopotamia) is considered the earliest calculating tool. Centuries later, in 1642, Blaise Pascal built the Pascaline, a mechanical adding machine.

The conceptual leap toward the modern computer came from Charles Babbage, an English mathematician who designed the Difference Engine (1822) and, more importantly, the Analytical Engine (1837) — a mechanical, general-purpose, programmable computer design, earning him the title “Father of the Computer.” Ada Lovelace, who wrote an algorithm for the Analytical Engine to compute Bernoulli numbers, is regarded as the world’s first computer programmer.

The electronic era began in the 1940s. ENIAC (Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer), completed in 1945 and operational in 1946, is widely cited as the first general-purpose electronic digital computer. This kicked off the five generally recognised generations of computers:

  • 1st Generation (1940s–50s): Vacuum tubes — bulky, hot, power-hungry (e.g., ENIAC, UNIVAC I).
  • 2nd Generation (late 1950s–60s): Transistors, invented in 1947 at Bell Labs by Bardeen, Brattain and Shockley — smaller, faster, more reliable.
  • 3rd Generation (1960s–70s): Integrated Circuits (ICs), pioneered by Jack Kilby (1958) and Robert Noyce (1959) — many transistors on one chip.
  • 4th Generation (1971–present): The Microprocessor — beginning with Intel’s 4004 in 1971 — put an entire CPU on a single chip.
  • 5th Generation (emerging): Artificial Intelligence, parallel processing, and natural-language systems.

Underlying this rapid miniaturisation is Moore’s Law — Gordon Moore’s 1965 observation that the number of transistors on a chip roughly doubles every two years. Alongside computing hardware, the convergence of computing, telecommunications, and broadcasting into a single digital ecosystem is what gave rise to the modern term “ICT.”

Key facts & history
  • Abacus (~2700 BCE) → Pascaline (1642) → Babbage’s Analytical Engine (1837)
  • Ada Lovelace: regarded as the first computer programmer
  • ENIAC (1945/46): first general-purpose electronic computer
  • 5 generations: Vacuum Tube → Transistor (1947) → IC (1958/59) → Microprocessor (1971) → AI
  • Moore’s Law (1965): transistor count doubles roughly every 2 years
6.1.3

Digital Devices

A digital device processes and stores data as discrete binary values (0s and 1s) rather than continuously varying signals — which is what distinguishes it from an analog device (like a mercury thermometer or a vinyl record player). This binary representation makes digital signals more resistant to noise and easier to copy, compress, and transmit without loss of quality.

Early “digital devices” in the consumer sense emerged slowly. IBM’s Simon Personal Communicator (1994) is often credited as the first true smartphone, combining a phone with a calendar, notepad, and touchscreen. It was Apple’s iPhone (2007), however, that popularised the modern smartphone era with its multi-touch interface and app ecosystem.

Today’s digital device landscape spans smartphones, tablets, smart TVs, e-readers, and an expanding category of wearables (smartwatches, fitness bands) and IoT (Internet of Things) devices — everyday objects (thermostats, doorbells, sensors) embedded with connectivity so they can collect and exchange data automatically. The process of converting an analog input (like a voice) into digital form is called Analog-to-Digital Conversion (ADC).

This explosion of digital devices has been made possible by decades of progress in semiconductor and microprocessor technology, allowing devices to shrink dramatically in size and cost while increasing in power — a direct continuation of the trend described by Moore’s Law.

Key facts & history
  • Digital = discrete binary data; Analog = continuous signals
  • IBM Simon (1994): first true smartphone
  • Apple iPhone (2007): modern smartphone era begins
  • IoT = everyday objects with embedded connectivity
  • ADC = Analog-to-Digital Conversion
6.1.4

Communication Systems

Modern telecommunication has roots in the 19th century. Samuel Morse developed the electric telegraph and Morse code, demonstrated in 1844, allowing the first practical long-distance electrical messaging. In 1876, Alexander Graham Bell patented the telephone, enabling real-time voice communication over wires. Guglielmo Marconi later pioneered wireless radio communication in the 1890s–1900s, removing the need for physical cables altogether.

The space age extended communication beyond Earth’s surface: Telstar 1 (1962), the first active communications satellite, enabled live transatlantic television and telephone signals. Meanwhile, mobile telephony advanced through distinct generations — 1G (early 1980s, analog voice only), 2G (digital voice and basic data), 3G (mobile broadband internet), 4G (high-speed LTE data), and 5G (commercially launched from 2019, offering very high speed, low latency, and massive device connectivity). The first handheld mobile call was made by Martin Cooper of Motorola in 1973.

Computer networking has its own parallel history. ARPANET, developed by the U.S. Department of Defense, went live in 1969 as the direct ancestor of the internet. Ray Tomlinson sent the first network email in 1971, choosing the “@” symbol to separate the username from the host computer’s name. The internet’s common language, TCP/IP, became the official standard in 1983. Finally, Tim Berners-Lee invented the World Wide Web in 1989 at CERN, giving the internet the hyperlinked, browsable interface used today.

Communication systems are described using terms like bandwidth (the data capacity of a channel over time) and switching method — circuit switching (a dedicated path for the whole call, as in traditional telephony) versus packet switching (data broken into packets routed independently, as on the internet).

Key facts & history
  • Telegraph (Morse, 1844) → Telephone (Bell, 1876) → Radio (Marconi, ~1890s–1900s)
  • Telstar 1 (1962): first active communications satellite
  • First handheld mobile call: Martin Cooper, Motorola, 1973
  • ARPANET (1969) → Email (Tomlinson, 1971) → TCP/IP standard (1983) → WWW (Berners-Lee, 1989)
  • Mobile generations: 1G (analog) → 2G → 3G (mobile internet) → 4G (LTE) → 5G (2019–)
6.2

Computer Hardware & Software

CPU, RAM, ROM, storage devices, input/output devices, system software, application software

6.2.1

Central Processing Unit (CPU)

The CPU is the “brain” of the computer, responsible for fetching, decoding, and executing instructions. It has two principal components: the Control Unit (CU), which directs the operation of the processor by interpreting instructions, and the Arithmetic Logic Unit (ALU), which performs all mathematical calculations and logical comparisons. The CPU also contains a small set of extremely fast storage locations called registers, used to hold data and instructions currently being processed.

The architectural blueprint behind most modern CPUs comes from mathematician John von Neumann, whose 1945 “First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC” described a design where both program instructions and data share the same memory and bus — now known as the von Neumann architecture. (The competing Harvard architecture uses separate memory for instructions and data.)

The physical CPU has shrunk dramatically over time. The first commercial microprocessor, Intel’s 4004, launched in 1971, packed an entire CPU onto a single chip for the first time. This single-chip approach defines the fourth generation of computers and underlies every modern processor, including RISC (Reduced Instruction Set Computer) designs like the ARM chips used in most smartphones.

Three terms describe CPU performance: clock speed (measured in GHz, the number of instruction cycles per second), cores (independent processing units on one chip — a multi-core CPU can execute multiple instruction streams simultaneously), and cache memory (very fast memory near the CPU that stores frequently used data to reduce delays). Every instruction a CPU runs follows the basic Fetch → Decode → Execute cycle.

Key facts & history
  • CU (control) + ALU (arithmetic/logic) = the two main CPU components
  • Von Neumann architecture (1945): shared memory for instructions & data
  • Intel 4004 (1971): first commercial single-chip microprocessor
  • Instruction cycle: Fetch → Decode → Execute
  • RISC (e.g., ARM) vs CISC: competing CPU design philosophies
6.2.2

RAM & ROM

RAM (Random Access Memory) is the computer’s primary working memory — it temporarily holds the programs and data the CPU is actively using. RAM is volatile, meaning all its contents are lost when the power is switched off. There are two main types: DRAM (Dynamic RAM), which is cheaper and denser but must be electrically refreshed thousands of times per second to retain data, and SRAM (Static RAM), which is faster and doesn’t need refreshing but is more expensive — making it ideal for CPU cache memory. The semiconductor DRAM cell that underlies modern RAM was patented by Robert Dennard at IBM in 1968.

ROM (Read Only Memory), by contrast, is non-volatile — it retains data even without power — and is used to store firmware such as the BIOS, the low-level startup instructions a computer needs every time it boots. ROM has evolved through several variants: PROM (Programmable ROM, written once), EPROM (Erasable PROM, erased using ultraviolet light and reprogrammed), EEPROM (Electrically Erasable PROM, erased electrically), and modern Flash memory, an evolved form of EEPROM used in USB drives and SSDs.

Key facts & history
  • RAM = volatile working memory; ROM = non-volatile firmware storage
  • DRAM (needs refreshing, cheaper) vs SRAM (faster, used for cache)
  • DRAM cell patented by Robert Dennard, IBM, 1968
  • ROM evolution: PROM → EPROM (UV-erasable) → EEPROM → Flash
6.2.3

Storage Devices

Secondary storage provides non-volatile, long-term data storage, unlike the CPU’s registers or RAM. Its history traces back to Herman Hollerith’s punched cards, used to tabulate the 1890 U.S. Census — an early form of mechanical data storage. Hollerith’s tabulating company eventually merged into what became IBM in 1924.

Magnetic storage dominated computing for decades. Magnetic tape was first used commercially with UNIVAC in the early 1950s and is still used today for archival backups. IBM introduced the first commercial hard disk drive, the RAMAC 305, in 1956. Portable magnetic storage followed with IBM’s 8-inch floppy disk in 1971, later shrinking to the familiar 5.25-inch and 3.5-inch formats.

Optical storage arrived with the Compact Disc (CD), commercially launched in 1982, followed by the DVD and Blu-ray, which used laser light rather than magnetism to read and write data. The most significant recent shift is the rise of Solid State Drives (SSDs), which store data in flash memory chips with no moving parts — making them faster and more durable than traditional HDDs, though historically more expensive per gigabyte.

Today, much storage has also moved off physical devices entirely into cloud storage — data kept on remote servers and accessed over the internet. Storage capacity itself is measured in a unit hierarchy: Bit → Byte → Kilobyte → Megabyte → Gigabyte → Terabyte, each roughly 1,000× (or 1,024×) the size of the one before.

Key facts & history
  • Hollerith punched cards (1890 Census) → led to founding of IBM (1924)
  • Magnetic tape (UNIVAC, early 1950s); IBM RAMAC HDD (1956); 8-inch floppy disk (1971)
  • CD (1982): first major optical storage format
  • SSD: flash memory, no moving parts, faster & more durable than HDD
  • Capacity units: Bit < Byte < KB < MB < GB < TB
6.2.4

Input & Output Devices

Input devices let users enter data and commands into a computer, while output devices present processed results back to the user. The standard computer keyboard layout, QWERTY, was designed in the 1870s by Christopher Latham Sholes for early typewriters and has persisted into the digital age largely due to its entrenched familiarity.

The computer mouse was invented by Douglas Engelbart in 1964 and famously demonstrated to the public during “The Mother of All Demos” in 1968, alongside early concepts of hypertext and video conferencing that were decades ahead of their time.

Common input devices include the keyboard, mouse, scanner (converts printed/handwritten documents into digital images), and microphone (converts sound into digital signals). Common output devices include the monitor (visual display) and printer (produces hard-copy paper output). Some devices, like touchscreens, function as both input and output simultaneously — accepting touch input while displaying visual output.

Key facts & history
  • Input devices: keyboard, mouse, scanner, microphone
  • Output devices: monitor, printer, speaker
  • QWERTY layout: Christopher Latham Sholes, 1870s
  • Computer mouse: Douglas Engelbart, 1964 (publicly demoed 1968)
  • Touchscreens: combined input + output device
6.2.5

System & Application Software

Computer software falls into two broad categories. System software manages the computer’s hardware and provides the platform on which everything else runs — its most essential example is the Operating System (OS). Application software, by contrast, is designed to help users perform specific tasks, such as word processors, spreadsheets, web browsers, and games.

One of the earliest operating systems, GM-NAA I/O, was developed for the IBM 704 in 1956. A major turning point came with UNIX, developed at Bell Labs starting in 1969, whose design principles still influence modern operating systems today, including Linux (created by Linus Torvalds in 1991) and Apple’s macOS. On the personal computer side, Microsoft released MS-DOS in 1981 for IBM-compatible PCs, followed by the graphical Microsoft Windows in 1985.

System software includes several specialised categories: utility software (performs maintenance like disk cleanup, antivirus scanning, and file compression), and device drivers (small programs that let the OS communicate with specific hardware, such as a printer or graphics card). Programming language translators are also system software: a compiler translates an entire high-level program into machine code before it runs, while an interpreter translates and executes code line-by-line.

Key facts & history
  • System software (manages hardware, e.g. OS) vs Application software (user tasks, e.g. Word)
  • GM-NAA I/O (1956): one of the first operating systems
  • UNIX (Bell Labs, 1969) → influenced Linux (Torvalds, 1991) and macOS
  • MS-DOS (1981) → Microsoft Windows (1985)
  • Compiler (translates all at once) vs Interpreter (line-by-line)
